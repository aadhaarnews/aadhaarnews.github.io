ATM hacks are less about losing a few lakhs & more about erosion of reputation.

  • May 25, 2017

Last year, Vijay Kumar, an Indian Army sepoy, got the shock of his life when he discovered a shortfall of Rs 2 lakh in his SBI salary account. Subsequently drawing out of transaction details and CCTV footage revealed Kumar’s card was “cloned” by fraudsters several kilometers from his base in Delhi Cantonment.

Kumar had never even travelled to Connaught Place where his card was used (or misused). Miscreants managed to get his bank details using a Malaysian-make card reader and a hidden camera (affixed to the ceiling of the rigged ATM, focused on the keypad) to capture the PIN. Card skimming – an old trick – is common in India, but on a waning curve as swindlers have moved on to more sophisticated technologies. Daring raids on ATM switches and payment gateways, in rising numbers, have turned fraudsters of yore into new-age hackers.

According to India Forensic, an educational & research organisation specialising in banking frauds, roughly 37,000 ATM fraud cases have been reported in the country since 2014. Industry experts estimate over 8,000 ATMs got infected by bugs last year – a good number on account of mishandling by third party handlers. Most banks outsource ‘ATM management’ (software updates, hardware service and others) to external agencies. ATMs get infected when third party service engineers use (virus) infected pen drives. Eight dacoity cases were reported in 2014, according to the National Crime Records Bureau, a couple of which included uprooting ATM machines and trucking them into the woods.

RANSOMWARE REARS UP

As a response to the recent ‘WannaCry Ransomware’ malware attack, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) asked banks to update software systems. There are unconfirmed reports of two south India based banks being infected, The Times of India stated recently. “Ample security measures have to be employed across all banks as customers use non-home ATMs as well. So only one bank with strong system or network may not really help. ATMs are easy targets,” explains Mahesh Patel, group CTO, AGS Transact Technologies, an ATM manufacturer.

Bankers, it appears, are not overly worried about individual ATMs as these hold only “a few lakhs of rupees” at any given time. But they are concerned about reputational risk associated with their ATMs getting hacked and plundered. Beyond that, banks are focused on securing pricier installations such as the ‘ATM or core banking switch,’ which link core accounts and clusters of ATMs. “Hackers are not interested in individual customers or ATMs anymore… they’re looking for larger gains and bigger play,” admits Johnson K Jose, head of IT at Federal Bank.

“Most ATM attacks happen at the (ATM) switch level these days… precisely for this reason, we don’t outsource switch-level operations to outsiders. But then securing individual ATMs is also important for banks… customers should feel safe when they transact on a particular bank’s ATM,” Johnson adds. ATM/debit card fraud complaints shot up 23% to 13,081 in 2015-16, as per the latest Banking Ombudsman Report. Of the 22,000 card-related complaints received, 8,300 pertain to failed ATM withdrawal transactions, short/non-dispensation and fraudulent removal of cash from customer accounts.

PHYSICAL ATTACKS

In September 2016, four youths uprooted an SBI ‘pass book updating machine’ thinking it was an ATM kiosk. Such instances of ‘ATM ramming’ are fairly common in Australia, where miscreants use tractors to pull out cash dispensers bolted to the ground. Physical attacks on ATMs are also on a rising curve in India. AnG India, an electronic security solutions company, receives over 100 alerts of “force” being exerted on ATMs every day. Of this, around 10 pertain to breakage or forced entry. “Not many ATMs have guards anymore… Banks mostly use e-surveillance systems,” says Pulkit Punj, director of AnG India. “Some kind of access restriction has to be there to keep away undesirable elements (near ATMs),” feels Mukul Shrivastava, partner – fraud investigation & dispute services, EY. “Also, there’s a lot of hardware (wires, connecting cables) lying around openly in ATMs. These have to be concealed properly… Such inlet points come handy for expert hackers to get through,” he warns.

DEFENCE, COUNTERMEASURES

Earlier, when banks requisitioned for new ATMs, they did not ask for security packages that came at extra cost. This has changed now. Banks have started demanding whitelisting modules, antiskimming devices and hard disk encryption software along with new ATMs. “Multiple banking touchpoints — from net banking to mobile banking and ewallets — are vulnerable to cyber attacks. So banks should not look at security in a piecemeal fashion,” says Navroze Dastur, MD of NCR Corporation, an ATM manufacturer.

Operating system updation would go a long way to secure ATMs from minor hacks. Whitelisting modules would ring-fence the core (software) by restricting unauthorised reworking of programmes using malwares. Strong safe boxes (Cen-1 safes) are being installed inside most new ATMs. Cen-1 strong boxes take approximately 35 minutes to cut open (a unit of measure for safe boxes) while the previously used UL291 boxes could be forced open in 15 minutes.

Instances of card skimming and cloning are likely to come down after the move to EMV (Europay, Mastercard, Visa) cards in two years. EMV or chip cards, according to experts, offer one of the most secure conduits for banking transactions. Some 300 million magstripe cards would be replaced with chip-embedded EMV cards by end-December 2018, as directed by RBI. If security experts are to be believed, six lakh new bugs are released into the “market” (thriving on internet) daily. Malwares such as Green dispenser, Suceful, Padpin and Tyupkin have cracked open ATMs of late. Some can be downloaded on a pen drive and their ‘spit-code’ sourced from Russian or American hack mafia for a price. Banks and security agencies will have to keep plugging the holes every single day to keep black hats at bay.
