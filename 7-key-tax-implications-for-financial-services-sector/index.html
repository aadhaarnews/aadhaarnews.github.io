7 Key Tax Implications For Financial Services Sector

  • Feb 3, 2017

Section 43D of the Income Tax Act recognises the principle of taxing income from sticky advances in the year in which it is received or credited to the books of account, says Mr Patel of EY.

In the previous budgets, the government sought to address certain concerns raised by the financial services industry. In the backdrop of demonetisation, broadening of tax base, ease of doing business in India, strengthening of financial sector and promote digital economy, Finance Minister Arun Jaitley presented the Union Budget for financial year 2017-18. This article seeks to capture some of the key implications of the Budget on the financial services and banking sector.

Currently, a deduction of 7.5 per cent of total income is allowed to Indian banks in respect of provision for bad and doubtful debts. The said deduction limit has been increased to 8.5 per cent of total income to give an impetus to the banking sector. However, the deduction limit of 5 per cent of total income for foreign banks operating in India continues to remain the same.

Section 43D of the Income Tax Act recognises the principle of taxing income from sticky advances in the year in which it is received or credited to the books of account. This benefit is currently available to scheduled banks, public/state financial institutions etc. In order to bring in parity within the lending sector and remove the hardship of paying tax even when income is not realised, such benefits have now been extended to the cooperative banks (excluding a primary agricultural credit society/primary co-operative agricultural) and rural banks.

In sync with India’s commitment to the OECD’s BEPS or base erosion and profit shifting project, a cap on deduction for specified interest payment has been prescribed. It is proposed that interest paid by an Indian company/permanent establishment (PE) of a foreign company to its associated enterprise (AE) shall be capped at 30 per cent of earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortisation (EBITDA) of such company/PE. However, an exemption is granted to interest payment up to Rs 1 crore and to banks and insurance companies. NBFCs or non-banking financial companies that are engaged in similar lending businesses and also regulated by the Reserve Bank of India are not exempted. Unlike the OECD guidelines (Action 4) that cover all interest payment, the proposed provisions are applicable only to interest paid to AEs or guaranteed by AEs.

After the Supreme Court decision in the case of Vodafone, the government introduced provisions to tax an offshore transfer of assets (indirect transfer) that substantially derive value from India. To address the concerns of foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) from a recent CBDT circular (that was withdrawn subsequently), it is clarified that the indirect transfer provisions does not apply to Category I and II FPIs registered with Sebi. However, Category III FPIs are not covered in this clarification.

Listing and trading of security receipts issued by a securitisation company or a reconstruction company under the SARFAESI Act will now be permitted on Sebi-registered stock exchanges. This is expected to enhance capital flows in to the securitisation industry and support banks in dealing with non-performing assets (NPAs).

A special tax regime in respect of offshore funds was prescribed in the year 2015. Given the onerous conditions, the scheme saw a lukewarm response from the industry. In continuation with certain relaxations provided last year, it is now clarified that in the year in which the fund is wound up, the condition of monthly average corpus of Rs 100 crore will not be applicable.

In continuation with the press release of 2015, a concessional withholding tax rate of 5 per cent has been prescribed for rupee-denominated bonds issued outside India. This amendment is applicable from April 1, 2016. Further, the concessional withholding tax rate of 5 per cent in respect of interest payable to FPIs in respect of their investments in government securities/rupee-denominated corporate bonds is extended to July 1, 2020. These concessions should boost debt investments in India.

The aforesaid reforms made by the finance minister are welcome and would go a long way in strengthening the financial service industry.
